Background

(4 sources)

Ethiopia's pastoralists occupy 61% of the country's land mass, with about 10 million people relying on livestock-based livelihoods in lowland areas. This traditional system faces mounting pressures from climate change, resource competition and land degradation which threatens its future viability and is leading to large-scale migration.

Pastoralism in Ethiopia represents both a cultural heritage and essential livelihood system that spans vast rangelands across the country. Pastoral communities occupy 61% of Ethiopia's total land mass, with 97% of Ethiopian pastoralists concentrated in lowland areas of Afar, Somali, Oromiya, and SNNPR regions. About 10 million pastoralists depend on livestock based livelihoods as their primary source of wealth and subsistence.

This traditional lifestyle faces significant challenges that threaten its sustainability. Ethiopian pastoralists now operate over increasingly degraded rangelands suffering from bush encroachment, with shrinking grazing areas due to population growth, agricultural expansion, land degradation, blocked migration routes, and resource-based conflicts. These communities face frequent droughts with high livestock mortality that often threatens the viability of pastoral livelihoods and leads to famine. Additional pressures include competition for water and pasture, underrepresentation in socio-economic and political activities, ethnic conflicts, and poverty.

The scale of climate-induced displacement among Ethiopian pastoralists has become severe, with communities experiencing catastrophic losses that force large-scale migration. Migration patterns have fundamentally changed as climate pressures intensify. The increase in frequency and length of drought cycles in Ethiopia have forced herders to move more frequently, often to new destinations for extended periods. However, pastoralist migration faces unique challenges in terms of recognition and protection. Unlike certain categories of migrants, pastoralist migration has not been formally recognised or protected, and administration borders are being drawn without considering pastoralist mobility needs, leading to inter-communal tensions, insecurity and conflict.

Ethiopia has 2.4m internally displaced people as of end of 2024.

Prevalence of Mental Health & Substance Misuse Problems

(3 sources)

Displaced communities in Ethiopia face high rates of mental health problems, including post-traumatic stress disorder, anxiety, and depression. These mental health problems are often the result of conflict-related trauma and harsh living conditions in settlement camps. Substance misuse is also rising among Ethiopian populations, with significant regional differences in prevalence.

Ethiopia faces a severe displacement crisis, with over three million internally displaced persons (IDPs) due to political conflicts (often relating to climate change), making it the third largest internal displacement situation globally.

The mental health burden is particularly acute in the Oromia region, due to displacement. Displaced individuals living in settlement camps experience the highest burden of mental health problems, often stemming from the initial trauma of conflict (with many having witnessed the killing of relatives or lost family members entirely) or the ongoing stressful living conditions in displacement settings. The most prevalent mental health conditions among displaced populations include post-traumatic stress disorder, anxiety, depression, and psychosis; with cases concentrated in camps around Sululta and Bishan Guracha.

Substance use presents as an additional challenge, with notable research showing that approximately 62% of Ethiopian university students use at least one substance, most commonly alcohol. Khat chewing affected roughly 23% of Ethiopian university students, with the highest use in Oromia region at 32%. Depression significantly increases the likelihood of substance use by approximately three times.

Cultural responses to mental health problems among displacement populations often involve traditional and religious approaches, with many associating mental illness with evil spirit possession or divine punishment for sins.

Interventions Used in Ethiopia and Similar Contexts

(6 sources)

Evidence-based interventions for displaced populations include cash transfer programs, screening and brief intervention systems, integrated mental health and substance use services, and culturally adapted trauma-informed approaches. These interventions emphasise community engagement, peer support, and multi-tiered prevention strategies that address both immediate needs and underlying trauma.

Research supports several key approaches for addressing mental health and substance misuse in displaced populations. Some examples include:

  1. Ethiopia's Productive Safety Net Programme provides cash transfers to reduce food insecurity from droughts and supported community relocation to safer areas where their lives and health are not threatened by climate disasters. These safety net programs address fundamental stressors that contribute to mental health deterioration in displaced populations. Such programs can help prevent the acute psychological trauma associated with sudden displacement while ensuring basic needs are met during transition.
  2. Screening, Brief Intervention and Referral to Treatment (SBIRT) systems provide an evidence-based framework that can effectively reduce unhealthy alcohol and drug use while addressing co-occurring mental health problems. These approaches should be integrated into routine clinical practice alongside brief motivational conversations, psychosocial support, and medication-assisted therapies for substance use dependence and withdrawal.
  3. Community-Based Care represents a critical approach for narrowing mental health and substance use treatment gaps in African contexts, with models classified into psychotherapeutic, social, lifestyle/physical health, economic, and psychopharmacological approaches. These interventions must address context-specific needs while adopting adaptable, scalable, and holistic approaches, with common recommendations including partnering with local organisations, implementing regular suicide prevention efforts, and customising digital tools to community needs.

Intervention Delivery

(3 sources)

Successful implementation of interventions requires multi-dimensional approaches with committed leadership, stakeholder buy-in, and adequate supervision, while addressing structural barriers through interdisciplinary coordination and community partnerships. Key challenges include ensuring local acceptability, managing resource constraints, and integrating services across humanitarian sectors.

Implementing mental health and substance misuse interventions in displaced populations requires multi-dimensional approaches that address the complex nature of these interconnected problems. Ethiopia's experience demonstrates that successful implementation depends on committed leadership by policymakers, stakeholder buy-in from critical partners, and calibrated integration that enables task sharing across different levels of healthcare providers.

Essential implementation factors include provision of adequate supervision and mentorship to ensure proper skills acquisition, program monitoring systems, and mobilisation of adequate financial and human resources to ensure quality and sustainability of interventions.

Context-specific adaptation represents a critical implementation challenge, requiring interventions that are feasible, relevant, and acceptable in local settings. Structural and systemic barriers pose significant implementation challenges that require coordinated responses across multiple sectors. Programs must address underlying causes including psychological trauma, exposure to potentially traumatic events, and difficulties meeting basic needs, which requires interdisciplinary coordination between humanitarian aid sectors.

Incorporating livelihood and recreational programming into substance use services necessitates collaboration between health sectors and economic development programs. Implementation strategies must ensure security and confidentiality for both implementers and participants while integrating local community leaders and traditional healing practices.

Service integration offers both opportunities and challenges for implementation, with potential to reduce structural stigma and increase service scope when substance misuse treatment is integrated into existing health systems like primary care or mental health services.

Summary

Ethiopia's pastoralists are facing increasing pressure from climate change resource competition and land degradation, leading to conflicts and large scale displacement. Conflict-related displacement has led to high rates of mental health and substance misuse problems among this vulnerable population. Ethiopia's response to climate-driven internal displacement increasingly recognises that mental health and psychosocial support are integral to recovery and resilience. Evidence-based interventions for displaced populations include cash transfer programs, screening and brief intervention systems, integrated mental health and substance use services, and culturally adapted trauma-informed approaches.
