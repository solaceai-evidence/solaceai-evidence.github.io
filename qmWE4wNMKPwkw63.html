Deep rEsearch Evaluation Protocol for Evidence Reviews

Building a tool to judge the quality of AI-generated deep-research evidence summaries

01 The six candidate domains

Domain 1Selection of Evidence
Domain 2Evidence Quality
Domain 3Fidelity of Data Extraction to Source
Domain 4Synthesis and Conclusions
Domain 5Structure and Usability
Domain 6Citations

A central conclusion: the domains are not independent. Synthesis depends on what came before it - pointing toward a stepwise, decision-tree structure rather than parallel scoring.

1

Selection of evidence

  • Prize specificity & directness to the user's context over 'diversity' per se (e.g. a US RCT may be useless for an Ethiopian question).
  • Broaden beyond geographic/linguistic to population, participant & topic diversity (sex, age, urban/rural).
  • Merge the overlapping range / breadth / study-design items; add a distinct relevance item.
  • Drop weak proxies (impact factor, citation count); weight robust local & national guidance.
  • Recency matters - hidden date cut-offs misrepresent the evidence base.
2

Evidence quality

  • Appropriate appraisal depends on question type - capture it early in the tool.
  • A gap: nothing currently links quality assessment into the conclusions, risking inflated conclusions that erode trust.
  • Aim for a pragmatic 'good enough' judgement - better than what AI systems do today (i.e. nothing).
  • The black-box problem: A clean risk-of-bias table can't be trusted to have actually been done.
3

Fidelity of data extraction to source

  • Felt critical: if extraction is fundamentally wrong, later domains are pointless.
  • Check that quality-relevant data (design, population, bias) was even extracted.
  • Plausibility to assess remains in question.
  • Could verify cheaply with light R/Python scripts (e.g. to check if the cited references actually exist).
4

Synthesis & conclusions

  • Re-check comparability of settings & populations across synthesised sources.
  • Split 'scope & applicability' from 'was the question answered?'
  • Plausibility to assess remains in question. Different items need different weights for different end-users.
  • 'Appropriateness of synthesis' was the hardest to operationalise — how would a policymaker judge a meta-analysis?
  • Safeguard against author spin & optimism propagating.
5

Structure & usability

  • Demand date/time transparency - search date, period covered, AI system/model publication date.
  • Show the process: how the question was decomposed, training data vs live search, what was excluded & why.
  • Plain language vs academic structure is use-case dependent - short summaries that expand on demand.
  • Length and structure are use-case and user dependent
  • Distrust 'flowery', over-sophisticated prose that masks thin substance.
6

Citations

  • A step-wise check: does the citation exist - is the reference real - are details accurate - is the claim supported - is the source any good.
  • Quantify accuracy as % correct (e.g. an 80% threshold) - debate on whether tolerance should be zero.
  • A fabricated citation behind a key claim is far worse than one for background.
  • Hallucinations may warrant their own item.

02 Cross-cutting themes

The question drives the report

What counts as good selection, synthesis and conclusions is conditioned by the prompt. The framework needs a preamble capturing question type and what matters for it.

Domains are not independent

Synthesis depends on what came before it - implying need for a stepwise or decision-tree structure.

Triage & 'stop early'

Fatal flaws in any domain should let an evaluator discard a report early, before spending effort on downstream judgement.

Define what's evaluated

Focus is on fully AI-generated reports (vs hybrid human-AI), with definitional work still needed on assumed user input.

Evaluation is contextual

DEEPER scores one report for one user, question and context - the same report can score differently for different people.

Rapid vs deeper two-tier concern

Caution around the potential for the rapid assessment to be perceived as lower standard. A two-tier design separates what's checkable from the report alone vs what needs external cross-checking which requires more time and resources.

'Some evidence vs none' debate

For many decision-makers the baseline is no evidence at all, so imperfect-but-useful has real value - but 'none beats biased'.

Feasibility & black-box limits

Many items may not be assessable because tools don't surface necessary information about sources, gaps or paywalled work. Can the tool know what it hasn't found?
